# Idea - Unicorn

## The Idea
> The unicorn is one of the most enduring mythological creatures — a white horse with a single spiraling horn, symbolizing purity, rarity, and magic. The word has since been borrowed by the startup world to describe something equally rare: a private company valued at over $1 billion.

## What Is a Unicorn?

### Mythological Unicorn
- Originates from ancient Greek and Roman natural history writings — first described not as myth, but as a real animal from India
- Became deeply embedded in European heraldry and folklore; the national animal of Scotland is a unicorn
- Core symbolism: **purity, untamability, and grace** — a unicorn could only be tamed by someone pure of heart
- Appears in Chinese mythology as the *qilin* — a similar creature symbolizing good luck and prosperity
- The single horn (*alicorn*) was believed to have magical healing properties, capable of neutralizing poison

### Startup Unicorn
- Coined by venture capitalist **Aileen Lee** in 2013 to describe US-based software startups founded since 2003 with a $1B+ valuation
- The term was chosen specifically because such companies were considered *mythically rare* at the time
- As of 2025, there are 1,000+ unicorns globally — the rarity has diminished, but the prestige hasn't
- Well-known examples: Airbnb, Stripe, SpaceX (pre-IPO), Grab, Gojek, Traveloka (Southeast Asia)

|------|-----------|

## Why It Matters

- The mythological unicorn captures something universal: the human desire to believe something *impossibly perfect* can exist
- The startup unicorn reframes that desire as ambition — it sets a north star that founders chase
- Both versions share a core trait: **rarity makes them valuable**. The moment unicorns become common, the magic fades (which is exactly what critics say about today's inflated startup ecosystem)
- For Southeast Asia specifically, unicorns like Gojek and Grab showed that the region could produce globally relevant tech companies — a turning point in regional startup culture
